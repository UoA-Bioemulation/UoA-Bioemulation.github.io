Pacemaker Validation

One of the benefits of an ExOoC is its customisability in a relatively small amount of time. Not only can this customisability be used for personalised organ models given specific patient data, but also is suitable for inducing disease states in the ExOoC.

For a heart-pacemaker interaction, by inducing some disease states in the heart and monitoring the interaction between them. Many of these disease states can be emulated through simple customisations to the models. In this section we will illustrate two specific disease states, Sionatrial (SA) Node Failure and Atrio-Ventricular (AV) Conduction Block.

Sinoatrial (SA) Node Failure

Modeling SA Node Failure can be accomplished by disabling the auto-rhythmic behaviour of the SA Node. In this case, the natural pacing rate from the heart drops from 100 bpm to the rate of the next highest auto-rhythmic node (the AV Node) at 60 bpm. Although this lower rate is not an imminent threat, due to the Action Potential propagation emanating from a location between the atria and ventricles the important timing delay between atrial and ventricular contractions is almost non-existant.

An example of testing a pacemaker when SA Node Failure occurs can be seen in the figure to the right. During the first 1 second the heart can be seen to be behaving normal, with atrial and ventricular events from the heart. At around 1.2 seconds we observe an abnormal behaviour as there is no atrial event. The pacemaker realizes the absence of atrial event and responds around 1.4 seconds by delivering a stimulus to the atrium (AP), followed by a ventricular stimulus (VP) at about 1.6 seconds after a timeout occurs. This process then continues indefinitely as the pacemaker maintains a suitable heart rhythm.

Atrioventricular (AV) Conduction Block

Similarly to modeling SA Node Failure, in order to model AV Conduction block the effect can be modeled by disabling the path connecting the AV Node and the Bundle of His. Here, there will be no propagation of Action Potentials from the atria to the ventricles. The atria will continue to beat auto-rhythmically at 100 bpm while the ventricles will beat slower, at around 30 bpm, the auto-rhythmic rate of the Bundle Branches. In this case, there is both an insufficient rate and extreme intervals between atrial and ventricular contractions.

An example of testing a pacemaker when AV Conduction Block occurs can be seen in the figure to the left. Normal heart activity occurs for the first 1.5 seconds, after which it can be seen that there is no natural propagation of the signal from the atria. Once a given time period has expired the pacemaker notices that the heart has not naturally paced the ventricles and so emits a pacing signal (VP). The atria continue to pace naturally (AS) with the pacemaker being forced to pace the heart (VP) after a predefined interval.
